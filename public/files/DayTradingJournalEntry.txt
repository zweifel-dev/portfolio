Understanding Charts and Indicators
Before we get into finding stocks to trade, we need to understand what we are looking at and for when trading stocks. Very first thing I learned was how to read the graphs for day trading.
Reading Candlestick Charts
Most common graph is based on candles set to certain intervals of time. Each candle is a 4 specific data points of all the orders that were filled in that bucket of time. For example, if I have a candle graph going from 9:30 am to 10:30am set at a 1-minute interval. I would see 60 candles, one for each minute. Time is on the x-axis while price is on the y-axis.
Now, like I mentioned, a candle shows 4 data points:
•	The opening price for that period (the first filled trade price for that bin)
•	The closing price for that period (the last filled trade price for that bin)
•	The highest price traded for that period
•	The lowest price traded for that period
The candle will have a box for the opening and closing price, have a wick (or sometimes called a shadow) going up to the highest price, and then a wick going down to the lowest price.
There could be tens of thousands to millions or billions of orders being placed in those candles between the highest and lowest price going up and down. The first thing I thought of is, why doesn't the graph show is the mean/average price for that specific bin. The volume of shares sold could be in one of the wicks, but the candle doesn't show the volume of where those are sold. I don't even know if having a volume average price for an individual wick really matters yet when doing analysis. What they do have though are other indicators.
Technical Indicators
VWAP (Volume Weighted Average Price): This is your Volume Weighted Average Price. It is an intraday metric meaning it resets every morning at 9:30am and will give the average price of the day taking volume into consideration. When this is graphed on the chart there are 3 lines. One line is the VWAP and then 1 standard deviation on each side. This can be useful to know for breakthrough momentums either up or down if it crosses one of the lines. It can also look for rebounds, support or resistance lines, if the price gets close to on of the lines and "bounces" back the other way.
EMA (Exponential Moving Average): This is similar and is the Exponential Moving Average. This is a type of moving average that assigns more weight to recent price data compared to older data. This makes it more responsive to price changes. You will typically see EMA followed by a number. That number refers to how many candles back that it is looking at to calculate the moving average.
Don't get EMA confused with SMA (Simple Moving Average), as EMA weighs closer to real time data exponentially more than its historical data. If you look online for the most common number to use with EMA, you will get a lot of variety from 4 to 200. The common ones though are 4,5, 8, 9, 10, 12, 20, 26, 50, 100, 200. The smaller the number the shorter term it is and will be quicker to catch movements and identify trends. Then the middle numbers like 20 to 50 are used for trend confirmation. Finally, the longer ones of 100 and 200 are for a broader direction and not really used for day trading but usually used for swing traders.
MACD (Moving Average Convergence Divergence): This is a trend momentum indicator that compares the relationship between two EMAs price. This is in its own chart and has 3 components, the MACD line, the signal line, and a histogram. The MACD line is calculated by subtracting the EMA-26 from the EMA-12. Showing you the difference between the two moving averages. The signal line is the EMA-9 line. The histogram will then show you the difference between the MACD line and the signal line. It visually highlights the convergence or divergence of the moving averages.
Some key points to note with the MACD is the crossover, when the MACD line crosses above the signal line, it can indicate a bullish trend (a buy signal). When the MACD line crosses below the signal, it can indicate a bearish trend (a sell signal). Another key point is the zero line and whether the MACD line crosses the zero line up or down, indicating that direction's momentum.
RSI (Relative Strength Index): Finally, the last indicator that I look at is the RSI. This is also a momentum indicator that measures the speed and magnitude of the price changes. It evaluates if the stock is overbought or oversold. The evaluation ranges from 0 to 100. Above 70 indicates overbought conditions and a potential price correction or downward trend could be coming. Below 30 is the opposite, it indicates an oversold condition, and a potential price rebound or an upward trend. When RSI is between 30 and 70, it is considered the neutral zone, with 50 meaning it is lacking any trend. This indicator is subjective on if it is a leading or lagging measure.
Now none of these indicators alone will determine the stock will move how you want. They are indicators and most are lagging indicators meaning they are behind in time what is happening compared to a leading indicator which happens first.
Candlestick Patterns
We can now look at candle patterns. A candle pattern is a set of candles forming a pattern that has been indicated as a precursor to predict price action. There are hundreds of patterns from doji, hammer, shooting star, morning star, evening star, hanging man, inverted hammer, three white soldiers, three black crows, spinning top, and many more. I have read more than I can count at this point and it's hard to memorize all of them. I bought a "cheat sheet" that I can post on the wall next to me with common ones.
The idea of technical analysis patterns is a controversial opinion in the trading community between day traders and swing traders. I have literally heard "Patterns like doji and dead cat bounce or whatever it is, is total BS. You need to use data points not astrology". Which is funny based on the names of these patterns, but what the individual is missing is that these patterns are representations of data. Remember that a candle is 4 data points (high, low, open, and close). So, these patterns they found are when these 4 data points aggregated with others line up in a certain way, statistically have acted in a certain way.
fivehundred.co did a study on candlestick patterns and their predictive power across 40 years of data on major stock indices and major large cap stocks. Though the patterns were more predictive 30-40 years ago close to 70% accurate, they are still in 2024 above 60% accurate. With accuracy determined by a candlestick pattern successfully detected to predict a move at least 1x the average true range (ATR) in the next 3 bars.
Sim Trading vs Real Trading
Paper trading or trading in a Sim (both are interchangeable terms) is a way to do trading with real data but no risk or gain. You are simulating "what would happen if I bought or sold X stock now". It tracks all of this on paper for you to see how you would have done if you were using real money. There are some draw backs I have found with this, but the benefits greatly outweigh the cost.
I started doing paper trading now in ThinkorSwim. I didn't transfer any money to my broker account or anything. I watched a couple of their educational tour videos on their platform. It all seemed easy enough. I did this for 2 days while continuing to read the material I have and finding all the nuances and terms I should know.
The Reality Check
The drawbacks of simulation trading become apparent once you start trading with actual capital. The emotional component (fear, greed, FOMO) doesn't exist in simulation mode. When you're trading with fake money, you make different decisions. You hold positions longer, take bigger risks, and stay calmer when trades go against you because your actual money isn't on the line.
But there's an even bigger problem that I didn't realize until I started trading with real money: price slippage. In simulation mode, when you place a market order, you get filled at exactly the price you see on the screen. It's perfect execution every time. But in the real market, there's slippage. You might see a stock at $10.50 and hit buy, but you actually get filled at $10.52 or $10.53, especially on fast-moving stocks with high volatility.
This creates terrible habits. In the sim, I'd set my stop loss at exactly 2% below my entry price because I knew I'd get filled at that exact price. But in real trading, that stop loss might get hit at 2.5% or 3% below my entry due to slippage, especially during volatile moments when the stock gaps down. So I started setting my stop losses tighter and tighter, thinking I was being smart about risk management. Wrong.
What ended up happening is I'd get into a position, the stock would move against me by just a tiny bit with normal price action and slippage, and I'd get stopped out way too early. Then I'd watch the stock reverse and hit my original profit target 30 seconds later. The slippage was eating into my planned risk tolerance and making me exit positions prematurely.
I paper traded for two days thinking I had a handle on it. Then I put real money in and realized I had learned absolutely nothing about the most important parts of trading: managing my own emotions AND accounting for real market conditions like slippage that don't exist in the sim.
Order Types and Risk Management
Now we can start trading, we know the types of orders and what the candles represent on the graph. Understanding order types is crucial before you start trading. It's not just "buy" and "sell", there's a whole set of configurations I can choose when looking at these.
TYPE	BUY	SELL
LIMIT	Buy at this exact price	Sell at this exact price
MARKET	Buy at the current market price	Sell at the current market price
STOP-LOSS	Buy when the price reaches a specific price. (Usually used on short positions)	Sell when the price reaches a specific price. Can be used to take profit at certain targets, or to get out of a position to lower your loss.
TRAILING-STOP	Buy order that triggers when the price moves by a certain amount ($, %, or ticks). The price moves with the market price. For Example: if stock X is at $20 and you think it will rise 5%, you put in a Buy trailing stop at 5%. If X moves to $21 (a 5% increase) then the buy order is triggered. If X moves down to $15, the buy order will move down with it to $15.75 (5% than market). Once it raises to $15.75 then it will trigger the buy. The trailing price will only move down and not up.	Sell order that triggers when the price moves by a certain amount ($, %, or ticks). The price moves with the market price. For Example: if stock X is at $100 and you set a trailing stop sell order at a distance of $10. Then the sell order initially would be at $90. If the stock rises to $110 though, then the stock rises to $100. Once it hits the price, the order is triggered. The trailing price will only move up, and not down.
Advanced Order Types
OCO Order: Conditional order that is placing two or more orders that are linked together. If one of the orders is filled, then the others in the group are automatically canceled. This can be used to set a profit target (an order to sell once you reach some profit) or a stop-loss (an order to sell once it dips to mitigate your loss)
Bracket Order: Specific type of OCO order that automatically places an OCO order after a position is opened. Pre-configured risk management tool. For example: You create a Bracket Order to Buy X at $5 with a take profit of 10% and a stop loss of 5%. Once the buy order is filled, it then automatically creates an OCO order to sell either at $5.50 (10% higher than $5) or a sell order at $4.75 (5% lower than $5). Only one of those sell orders will fill, and the other gets canceled.
Risk Management Philosophy
When creating a buy order, you want to enter the position knowing your risk and reward. For example, if the stock is $1.0000/share and you are setting yourself to a 1:2 risk ratio (risk 1 to reward yourself 2), and you put your stop loss risk at 5%, then your risk is for the share to drop to $0.9500/share, and your profit target is $1.1000/share (5c:10c or 1:2). With a 1:2 risk/reward ratio, then your accuracy only needs to be at 33% to break even. Obviously, you want to be higher than this, and not each trade is the same dollar amount risk.
At the beginning, I just want to make a profit (be green) on my trades. I have noticed that my first trades I have not thought this through enough even though learning it. I end up buying an order thinking it will go up with the momentum, and it drops only a little bit, and I cut my losses instead of staying in until my stop loss. Where if I would have waited 30s more, it went back up and past my profit target. Other times, I buy, and it dips down past my stop loss target, and I sit there too long before taking a loss and now I have lost more than I should have.
ATR Strategy for Stop-Loss and Profit Targets
This gets to the point, how do you know what to set your stop loss to and profit. That is something I am still trying to figure out the best strategy. There are multiple different opinions based on "support" and "resistance" levels (pre market's highs and lows, VWAP and its bands, trend lines on peaks, etc.). One metric I am looking at is a stock's ATR (average true range). This measures volatility by averaging price fluctuation over a specified period. This is typically a 14-day period. Knowing how much a stock can move up and down can help you know your exit position, so your stop loss doesn't get hit too soon. ATR is considered a lagging indicator though, meaning that it is calculated using historical price data and not a prediction of the future and won't know about volatility spikes or drops.
This is where I will use the ATR and draw a horizontal line on the chart at my entry position minus the ATR and another line above the position 2x the ATR. This will be my enter and exit positions. If open or close is past one of these thresholds (not the wicks) then I will exit.

The next is finding a stock we want to trade in. This has been one of the harder parts so far in this journey. Stock Price Considerations
First is price of the stock, and I will bucket them into different bins:
•	$1.00 or less
•	$1.00 to $5.00
•	$5.00 to $10.00
•	$10.00 to $20.00
•	$20.00 to $50.00
•	$50.00 to $100.00
•	$100 or more
The cheaper the stock is, the more shares you can buy based on your initial investment. That doesn't mean though you will see more profit or loss though. It varies but in those stocks under $5 I have seen are trading in fractional pennies (example: $1.2473/share). So, they could be moving quite a lot within those fractional of pennies compared to the share that is $8.00 which is moving in cents. The lowest amount a stock can move is called a tick.
Let's go back to the $1.00 stock. If you are taking a profit of $0.10/share, that doesn't really seem worth it to get into day trading. This is the key though and where it "takes money to make money". Let's say you bought the stock at $1.00 with your 5 cent stop loss and 1:2 risk/reward ratio. It then hits $1.10, and you sell your entire position. If you bought 100 shares, it would have originally cost you $100, and you are exiting with $101. In this example you were risking $0.50 for a profit of $1.00. Now let's say you had bought 10,000 shares instead. You are now profiting $100 and risking $50, but it would have required you to have $10,000 buying power to get into the investment in the first place.
Key Screening Criteria
Knowing these filters is called screeners, setting up a screener to find stocks that meet what you are looking for. Let's get back to finding which stock to even look at for trading. The big criteria that I am looking for is relative volume and gap movement.
Relative Volume: The first is an easy concept, is the stock trading more than it normally does? This means if the average volume of the stock is that it does 500k trades, and today it is now at 10M, then the relative volume is up by 20x.
Gap Movement: The next is the gap movement, which looks at the previous day close and current price (either from afterhours, premarket, or intraday trading) and the difference of the price, either up or down. You want a high relative volume and a large difference in gap. This way you know that a stock is moving.
The other 3 criteria I see day traders put in are average volume, market cap, and float.
Average Volume: Average Volume is the normal volume amount of trade for that stock. If this is too low, then it doesn't matter if the relative volume is 10x since it is only 10x a small number. I look for regular volume over 500K for a day.
Market Cap: Market cap of a stock is somewhat related to price. I stick with micro and small cap stocks which is under $2B. The reason this is important is because that is generally where retail traders are regularly trading where institutional traders who have larger capital to work with are working with the larger priced stocks.
Float: The last criteria is float. This one has been tricky for me to understand. Float refers to the number of shares of a company stock that are freely available for public trading. When first learning, I was thinking that I wanted this to be high. More shares to trade I would have thought would be a good thing. A stock with a lower float, meaning fewer shares are available, tends to be more volatile though (which is what day traders want). Float is calculated by taking the total number of stocks for a company and subtracting those held by insiders, restricted shares (like employees), and shares held by institutional investors. Float rotation then refers to how quickly the shares in a stock's float change hands throughout the day. A higher float rotation suggests that a stock is actively traded. This gives a greater potential for intraday trading. Anything below 100M float is good but finding a stock under 50M or ideally under 10M would be much better.

Tax Implications
I want to discuss two more important points and they both have to do with taxes. Need to understand the implications of day trading either by profit or by loss to your taxes at the end of the year.
The Wash Sale Rule: A Day Trader's Nightmare
The first is the Wash Sales Rule. This is if you trade the same stock within 30 days, it is subject to the wash sales rule. And you cannot claim the losses as deductions on your taxes. Currently, there is a max amount of trade losses you can claim each year set at $3000. If you have more than that, it rolls over to the next year to claim.
For day traders, this creates a potentially devastating scenario. Let's say you made multiple trades on stock XYZ: one that gave you a profit of $4000 and another that gave you a loss of $5000. When it's time to file taxes, you are taxed on the $4000 profit, but you can only deduct $3000 of the $5000 loss (and only if the wash sale rule doesn't apply). You're still out the additional $2000 loss, but the IRS still wants taxes on your gains. The wash sale rule can disallow your losses entirely if you rebuy the same stock within the 30-day window, meaning you could end up paying taxes on money you didn't actually make.
Trader Tax Status: The Solution
The way around this is to file for Trader Tax Status: Mark-To-Market (475 MTM Election). What this does is allows traders to aggregate their profit and losses as if they were all done on the last day of the year. In the previous example then you would be at -$1K instead of $4K in taxable income and a loss deduction of $3k.
With Trader Tax Status, you're treated as a business rather than an investor. This means:
•	No wash sale rules apply
•	No $3000 annual loss limitation
•	You can deduct trading-related expenses (platform fees, data costs, home office)
•	All gains and losses are treated as ordinary income (no capital gains treatment)
To qualify, you need to trade frequently (typically 720+ trades per year), hold positions for short periods, and derive substantial income from trading activity. The IRS doesn't publish exact requirements, so it's somewhat subjective.
The Critical Timing Issue
Something important to note though with this is that you need to file ahead of time that you will be doing this when you file for taxes the previous year. It is June 2025 right now and I already filed taxes for 2024, so it is too late for me to file for Trader Tax Status for the 2025 year. I will need to file in December of this year electing for Trader Tax Status for the 2026 year. You have to make the Section 475(f) election by April 15th of the year before you want it to take effect.
This timing requirement is crucial for day traders. The difference between paying taxes on phantom gains versus your actual net results can be thousands of dollars, making proper tax planning essential for anyone serious about day trading.
